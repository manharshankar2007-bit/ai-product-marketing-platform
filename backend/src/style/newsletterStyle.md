# Newsletter Style Guide

This is the editorial source of truth for every newsletter generated by this
application. It is derived entirely from three real, approved company
newsletters — every rule below traces back to an observable pattern in one
of them. Where this document seems to disagree with itself, that's not an
error: it means the real source newsletters didn't fully agree with each
other either, and both variants are documented rather than papered over.

This is a **living document**. It should be revised after comparing real
generated newsletters against real approved ones.

**Source material** (see `docs/examples/`):

- `whats-new-example.md` — "One ID to Track Everything" (What's New)
- `rider-active-task-webhook-example.md` — "Rider Active Task Webhook"
  (What's New — a second real example, used to cross-check patterns against
  the first)
- `whats-new-cod-handling.md` — "COD Handling Logics for Riders" (What's
  New — a third real example, cross-checked against the first two; same
  structure, adds a 4-item feature grouping under one release)
- `coming-soon-example.md` — "System Changes effective from July 1" (Coming
  Soon — grouped sub-style, see Section 5.3)
- `coming-soon-map-routing.md` — "Map Based Routing" (Coming Soon —
  single-feature sub-style, see Section 5.3; labeled "Coming Soon" in its
  subject line but its body follows the What's New structure)

## 1. Purpose

There are **two distinct newsletter types with two distinct purposes**.
They are not variations of one template — they exist to do different jobs,
and this maps directly to feature `status` from the extraction pipeline
(`shipped` / `in_progress` → What's New; `planned` → Coming Soon).

**What's New** exists to announce something that has already shipped or is
actively rolling out. Its job is to tell operationally-focused readers what
changed, why it changed, and what it means for how they use the product
*today*. Evidence: both What's New examples describe capability that is
already live ("Reference ID is now officially the first, fixed... column",
"You can now subscribe to webhooks...") and close with a "What This
Means To You" / "Why This Matters To You" section addressed at the reader's
current workflow.

**Coming Soon** exists to prepare readers for a change that has not
happened yet, so nobody is caught off guard when it does. Its job is to
announce the change, explain the reasoning, and give enough operational
detail that the reader can act before the effective date. Evidence: the
Coming Soon example is anchored to a specific future effective date ("10
changes take effect on 1 July"), and repeatedly frames content in future
tense ("will be automatically marked," "will no longer... be able to,"
"Existing Enterprise accounts are backfilled... ahead of this so nobody is
caught off guard").

## 2. Audience

The real newsletters are written for **internal, operationally-focused
platform users** — never external customers, never a general public. The
addressed "you" in these newsletters is someone who manages riders, orders,
billing, or accounts inside the product day to day. Based on who each
piece of content is written for:

- **Operations / Admin teams** — the "One ID" newsletter is explicitly
  addressed to "Admins" tracking orders.
- **Fleet / Rider Operations teams** — the Webhook newsletter is addressed
  to teams managing "your riders" and integrating "your systems."
- **Business Account Admins / Finance & Compliance stakeholders** — the
  Coming Soon newsletter addresses business-side KYC, GST, billing, and
  account-hierarchy owners.
- **Internal Product / Support teams** — implied recipients for
  awareness, per the Bcc list on the real Webhook email (Product Updates,
  Pidge Tech, BD Rockstars, Customer Success, Network Ops).

---

## 3. Tone

Derived from actual sentence patterns in the source material, not generic
adjectives:

- **Direct and concrete over promotional.** "Reference ID is now
  officially the first, fixed, and non-editable column" — states the
  mechanical fact, not an adjective about how great it is.
- **Explains the "why," not just the "what."** Nearly every item in every
  example pairs a factual statement with a plain reason clause: "Completed
  or terminal tasks are excluded to ensure signal over noise." The "why"
  is stated causally, in the same breath as the "what" — it is not a
  separate marketing pitch.
- **Confident but not exaggerated.** No superlatives appear anywhere in
  the three source documents except the one explicit exception the
  document itself uses stylistically: *"One ID to Track Them All!"* and
  *"July 01: The Rules Change. No Exceptions"* — punchy taglines are
  allowed to be a little dramatic; body copy is never allowed to be.
- **Never sales/marketing language.** None of "game-changing,"
  "revolutionary," "seamless," "excited to announce," or similar language
  appears anywhere in the three real newsletters. Not once.

---

## 4. Voice

Write as a Product Marketing Manager communicating product changes to
internal teams who will operationally use these features — colleagues, not
customers. The real newsletters never say "thank you for being a valued
customer" or address an external buyer; they say things like "your
systems," "your riders," "Admins," matching an internal operator, not a
consumer. Avoid sales language entirely — see Section 9.

---

## 5. Structure — Two Separate Newsletter Types

These are two distinct documents. Do not merge What's New content and
Coming Soon content into one document. Coming Soon itself has two valid
body sub-styles depending on content shape — see Sections 5.2–5.4 — but
picking between them is still about matching the release's own content,
never about combining both newsletter types together.

### 5.1 "What's New" structure

1. **Title** — the feature/theme name. Evidence is mixed on whether this
   is a distinct line from the tagline: the Webhook example has an
   explicit plain-text title line ("Rider Active Task Webhook") separate
   from its intro paragraph, while the "One ID" example opens directly on
   an italicized tagline with no separate title line above it. Treat
   `Title` as required (it is always at minimum embedded in the subject
   line and the tagline) and treat a separate plain-text title line as
   optional — both variants are attested in real, approved newsletters.
2. **Tagline** — short, punchy, italicized. e.g. *"One ID to Track Them
   All!"*
3. **Intro paragraph** — context: what problem existed, what's now
   solved. e.g. "In most of the places, different sort of ID's has been
   used as the primary tracking identifier. This difference often caused
   confusion..."
4. **Why We Built This** (section label observed verbatim as both "Why
   We Built This?" and "Why This Matters To You" across the two real
   examples — see Section 6 note on label variance)
5. **What's New!** — numbered or bulleted list of features, bold lead-in
   per item (both list styles are attested — see Section 8).
6. **What This Means To You** — grouped by audience/impact, not a
   per-feature repeat of what's already been said.
7. **What's Next** — short teaser, optional. Only attested once ("Something
   exciting is coming soon - Stay tuned !!"); omit when there is nothing
   concrete to tease.
8. **Footer** — company address + "VISIT WEBSITE" link.

### 5.2 "Coming Soon" — grouped sub-style

Evidence: `coming-soon-example.md` ("System Changes effective from July 1").
Use this sub-style when a release bundles **multiple, unrelated changes**
into one release note — see Section 5.4 for how to tell the two Coming
Soon sub-styles apart.

1. **Title** — theme name (in the observed example, expressed entirely
   through the tagline and intro; no separate plain title line was
   present in the source).
2. **Tagline** — short, punchy, italicized. e.g. *"July 01: The Rules
   Change. No Exceptions"*
3. **Intro paragraph** — scope of what's changing and when. e.g. "10
   changes take effect on 1 July across Verification & KYC, Account
   Structure, Billing & Invoicing, and Cash Automation."
4. **Why This Is Changing**
5. **Key Changes** — grouped by category, bold heading per item,
   explanatory body per item. **Important note:** the real source
   document does not contain the literal heading text "Key Changes" — it
   flows directly from "Why This Is Changing" into bold category headings
   (e.g. "Verification & KYC," "Account Structure & Hierarchy," "Billing &
   Invoicing") and then bold item headings underneath each. Per the
   original task brief, fixed section labels are inserted by application
   code rather than generated by the AI writer, so this is consistent —
   just note that the label itself was not literally observed in the
   source, only the grouped-category-then-item content shape was.
6. **Footer** — company address + "VISIT WEBSITE" link. No "What's Next"
   teaser in this structure — not observed in the source, and it wouldn't
   make sense (a Coming Soon piece describing multiple bundled changes
   shouldn't tease a further, unspecified thing beyond them).

This sub-style always closes directly on the last category's changes —
never on a reader-impact section.

### 5.3 "Coming Soon" — single-feature sub-style

Evidence: `coming-soon-map-routing.md` ("Coming Soon: Map Based Routing").
This is a real, approved newsletter that uses the "Coming Soon" subject
line, but its body is structurally identical to the What's New structure
in Section 5.1 (Title / Tagline / Intro / **Why We Built This?** /
**What's New!** / **What This Means To You** / **What's Next**) — not the
grouped, category-then-item shape of Section 5.2. Use this sub-style when
a release announces **one cohesive feature or theme** (even if that
feature has several facets/items) that simply hasn't fully shipped yet,
rather than several disparate changes.

The company's own usage shows "Coming Soon" labels the *timing* (not yet
fully live), not a fixed body structure — the body structure to use still
depends on whether the content is one cohesive feature or several bundled
changes.

### 5.4 Telling the two Coming Soon sub-styles apart

- **Single-feature** (Section 5.3): one feature/theme, described through a
  small number of closely related facets — e.g. Map-Based Routing's three
  items (Forward/Reverse Orders, Hover Insights, On-the-Fly Route
  Construction) are all facets of the same map-routing capability. Closes
  on reader impact, like What's New.
- **Grouped** (Section 5.2): several genuinely unrelated changes bundled
  into one release note — e.g. Verification & KYC, Account Structure,
  Billing & Invoicing, and Cash Automation are unrelated areas, not facets
  of one feature. Closes directly on the last category, no reader-impact
  section.

**These structures are not interchangeable.** What's New and the
single-feature Coming Soon sub-style both close on reader impact ("What
This Means To You"); the grouped Coming Soon sub-style never does — it
closes directly on the last category's changes.

## 6. Headings

- **Section headings are Title Case**, every word capitalized, including
  short words: "What's New!", "Why We Built This?", "What This Means To
  You" (note "To" capitalized), "Why This Is Changing" (note "Is"
  capitalized).
- **Category headings** (Coming Soon's groupings) are also Title Case:
  "Verification & KYC," "Account Structure & Hierarchy," "Billing &
  Invoicing."
- **Item headings** (the bold lead-in on each feature/change) are
  predominantly Title Case too: "The New Universal Primary Key," "Real-Time
  Task Visibility," "Parent Accounts Become View-Only Holding Entities."
  One real exception exists — "Mandatory Aadhaar Verification **for**
  Riders" keeps "for" lowercase — so treat this as the dominant pattern,
  not an absolute rule with zero exceptions.
- **Two different section labels for the same conceptual section** are
  both attested for the "why" section in What's New pieces: "Why We Built
  This?" (One ID) and "Why This Matters To You" (Webhook). Since these
  are fixed labels inserted by application code rather than by the AI
  writer (per the original task scope), this guide documents both as
  valid — it is an application-level decision which literal label a given
  What's New piece uses, not a writing-style decision.
- **Body/sentence text** is normal sentence case throughout — headings are
  the only place Title Case applies.

## 7. Feature Formatting

For each feature, this is how the real newsletters present it:

- **Title** — must be the actual, specific feature name. Never a generic
  bucket label. Real examples: "The New Universal Primary Key," "Bank
  Verification Required Before Any COD Cash Is Paid Out." Never something
  like "Key Features" or "Benefits" used as an item title (those words
  only ever appear as *section* labels, never as a feature's own name).
- **Description** — plain, concrete prose stating what changed. No
  hedging, no throat-clearing.
- **Business value** — in every real example, this is *folded directly
  into the description*, not a separate labeled line. It shows up as a
  cause-effect clause in the same sentence or the next one: "...to ensure
  signal over noise," "...so mismatches and incorrect invoices" are
  prevented. Do not add a separate "Business Value:" label — state it as
  part of the feature's own prose.
- **User impact** — in the real examples this mostly lives in its own
  section ("What This Means To You" / "Why This Matters To You"), which
  synthesizes impact across multiple features rather than repeating it
  under each individual item. When writing per-feature user impact, avoid
  duplicating exactly what that section will already say (see Section 8,
  "never repeat the same information across two sections").
- **Navigation** (when present) — rendered exactly as observed in the
  Webhook example:

  ```
  Go to Settings → Communications → Rider
  ```

  **This is an important correction to the originally assumed notation.**
  The task brief that requested this guide described the format as
  `"Navigation → X > Y > Z"` — a mix of arrows and `>` separators with a
  literal "Navigation" label. The actual real newsletter uses **only
  right-arrows (→) between every segment**, no `>` characters at all, and
  no "Navigation" label — instead it reads as a plain instruction: "Go to
  [segment] → [segment] → [segment]." Per the rule that examples win over
  assumptions, use the real notation:

  - Prefix: `Go to `
  - Segments joined by ` → ` (space, arrow, space)
  - Use the verbatim path from extraction — never paraphrase a segment
    name.

- **Steps** (when present) — a numbered list immediately following the
  navigation line, using the verbatim steps from extraction. Real
  example:

  ```
  1. Select Rider Active Task trigger
  2. Enable the webhook configuration
  3. Add:
     - Webhook URL
     - Auth Token
  4. Test the webhook
  5. Save the configuration once the test is successful
  ```

  Never reword or reorder a step.

## 8. Writing Rules

- **Never invent information not present in the extracted data.**
- **Never mention or reference null fields.** If a feature has no
  `limitations`, `configuration`, `rolloutNotes`, etc., omit them entirely
  — do not write "no limitations were specified" or any equivalent filler.
  None of the three real newsletters ever mention the *absence* of
  something; they only ever state what exists.
- **Never repeat the same information across two sections.** The real
  newsletters state a fact once, in the section where it belongs (the
  feature description covers the "what," the impact section covers the
  "so what") — they do not restate the same sentence twice in different
  words.
- **Avoid unnecessary adjectives.** Let the feature's actual mechanism
  carry the sentence. Compare the real "Reference ID is now officially the
  first, fixed, and non-editable column" (a mechanical fact) to what an
  adjective-driven rewrite would look like (see Section 11).
- **Do not overuse bullet points.** The real newsletters use short prose
  paragraphs for single ideas (e.g. every paragraph in "Why This Is
  Changing") and reserve numbered/bulleted lists for genuinely
  sequential steps or genuinely parallel feature/change items — never for
  a single idea that could just be a sentence.

## 9. Formatting Rules

- **Paragraph spacing:** one blank line between paragraphs, never dense
  blocks. Every real example is written this way — short paragraphs (often
  2–4 sentences), never a wall of text.
- **Bold usage:** feature titles, item lead-ins, and key terms only —
  never full sentences. e.g. `**Reference ID**` (a key term) and
  `**The New Universal Primary Key:**` (an item lead-in), but the
  explanatory sentence that follows is never bolded.
- **Bullet/numbered-item formatting — two attested patterns, both valid:**
  - *Bold lead-in + colon, explanation continues same block* (One ID
    example): `**The New Universal Primary Key:** Reference ID is now
    officially...`
  - *Bold heading on its own line, explanation as the paragraph below, no
    colon* (Webhook and Coming Soon examples): `**Inactive Riders Marked
    Automatically At Month-End**` followed by a new paragraph.

    Use either, but be consistent within a single newsletter — don't mix
    both patterns in the same list.
- **Lists — numbered vs. bulleted:** Numbered lists are used for two
  purposes: (a) procedural steps — **always** numbered, never bulleted, in
  every real example that has steps, and (b) a small set of substantial,
  individually-explained feature/impact items when the writer wants to
  convey a specific count (e.g. "1. ... 2. ... 3. ..." in "What's New!").
  Bulleted lists (•) are used for the same kind of parallel item set when
  no specific count needs emphasizing (the Webhook example uses bullets for
  the identical structural role that the One ID example fills with
  numbers). Short, homogeneous, single-line facts (e.g. "What changes?" in
  the Webhook example) are also numbered, but with no bold and no
  per-item explanation — just the fact itself.

---

## 10. Things to Avoid

- **Marketing buzzwords** — "game-changing," "seamless," "revolutionary."
  Zero instances across all three real source newsletters.
- **AI-generated clichés** — "in today's fast-paced world," "we're
  excited to announce." Zero instances across all three real source
  newsletters.
- **Speculation beyond what the source document states.**
- **Redundant wording** — saying the same thing two different ways in the
  same section (see Section 8).

## 11. Good vs. Bad Examples

Each "Good" line below is pulled verbatim from the real source files. Each
"Bad" line is a rewritten, generic/AI-cliché version written to illustrate
the contrast — it does not appear in any real newsletter.

### Example 1 — Feature heading

**Good** (from `whats-new-example.md`):
> **The New Universal Primary Key:** Reference ID is now officially the
> first, fixed, and non-editable column across all Pidge Powered pages
> (Overview, Ready to Ship, On Ground, At Warehouse, and Completed).

**Bad** (invented, for contrast):
> **Exciting Improvements to IDs:** We're thrilled to announce a
> game-changing update that revolutionizes how you track everything
> across the platform!

### Example 2 — "Why we built this" style explanation

**Good** (from `coming-soon-example.md`):
> Pidge ends up holding cash that's owed back to a business; without a
> verified account to send it to, that money has nowhere to go. Finance
> will not process the money unless bank information is available and
> verified in system.

**Bad** (invented, for contrast):
> In today's fast-paced logistics world, seamless cash flow is more
> important than ever. We're excited to bring you a revolutionary new
> verification system that takes your financial experience to the next
> level!

### Example 3 — Navigation / steps block

**Good** (from `rider-active-task-webhook-example.md`):
> Go to Settings → Communications → Rider
>
> 1. Select Rider Active Task trigger
> 2. Enable the webhook configuration
> 3. Add: Webhook URL, Auth Token
> 4. Test the webhook
> 5. Save the configuration once the test is successful

**Bad** (invented, for contrast):
> Simply navigate to your settings area and explore the communications
> options, where you'll find everything you need to seamlessly configure
> your brand-new webhook in just a few easy steps!

### Example 4 — Subject line

**Good** (from `coming-soon-example.md`):
> 🔔 Coming Soon: System Changes effective from July 1

**Bad** (invented, for contrast):
> 🚀✨ You Won't Believe These Game-Changing Updates We Just Shipped!
